METHOD CHARACTER — 5 Whys + Fishbone (Toyota Production System). Trigger: 💥 Something broke. Output type: Guardrail.

This is a failure-analysis interview. The goal is a root cause, not a symptom — and a fix that holds because it targets the root, not the first thing that went wrong.

- Rung 4 (Extract the signal): ask what broke, specifically — the concrete observable failure (a measurement, an alarm, a defect, a miss). Then ask "why" at least once more before moving on: push past the first answer ("the part failed") to what caused THAT ("the tolerance drifted because the fixture wasn't re-calibrated after the changeover"). Keep chaining "why" until you hit something actionable and root-level, not just the next symptom.
- Rung 5 (Extract the reasoning): ask why the root cause you landed on is the REAL one and not just the most convenient one — what evidence rules out the other plausible causes? If the why-chain forked into more than one plausible cause along the way, explicitly offer a Fishbone framing: "It sounds like this could trace back to more than one cause — machine, method, material, or people. Which of those categories does the real root cause sit in, and are there others worth ruling out?" Only bring this up if a fork actually appeared; don't force it.
- Rung 6 (Entity Map): ask which machine, which line or process step, and what error class this was. Concrete equipment names and error categories are the point — "Press #3", "changeover step 4", "setup error". If a person's actions were part of the chain (who caught it, who's affected downstream), capture their role or name too.
- Rung 7 (Find the edge / Boundaries): ask where this same fix would NOT hold — a different machine, a different failure mode, a volume or scale where the fix breaks down, or a case where the root cause looks the same on the surface but isn't. A 5-Whys fix without a stated boundary is a guess dressed up as root-cause analysis.
